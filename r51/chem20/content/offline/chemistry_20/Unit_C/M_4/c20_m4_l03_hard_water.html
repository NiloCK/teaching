Lesson 3—Solubility

 

Different Types of Hard-Water Buildup

 

Calcium is present in tap water in the form of calcium carbonate. Even though calcium carbonate is generally insoluble in water, the concentration is so low that it remains dissolved in solution. Depending on the context, hard-water buildup can arise in different ways:

  • hot-water buildup (found in pipes, kitchen appliances, bathroom fixtures, etc.)
    Calcium carbonate is a unique ionic solid—it becomes less soluble when the water temperature increases! Consider boiling hard water in a pot on the stove. As the water heats up and reaches a boil, calcium carbonate precipitates out of the solution, forming a residue on the bottom and sides of the pot. This is similar to the hard-water buildup that occurs in pipes and other fixtures that use hot water.

  • soap scum (found on dishes, shower curtains, etc.)
    Soaps contain organic acids that react with magnesium and calcium in hard water to form an insoluble salt. This can result in the residue commonly found on dishes and shower curtains.

  • evaporation (found on surfaces where hard water evaporates)
    When hard water evaporates, all dissolved solutes will be left behind as the water dries up. If you have ever cleaned a glass table top with tap water (and no other cleaning agents), you have likely observed the spots associated with hard-water evaporation.

Do you have a pot or fixture that has hard-water buildup? It is easy to remove hard-water calcium deposits using an acid solution, such as vinegar. If the buildup is on a flat surface, cover the surface with vinegar for several minutes and the buildup will dissolve. If the surface is upright, you could scrub the surface with a paper towel soaked in vinegar. Commercial products are also available for this process and can usually do the job more quickly.

Module 3—Behaviour of Gases

Respiratory Therapist Interview

 

Student: So you’re a respiratory therapist. What’s that?

Respiratory Therapist: A respiratory therapist provides services to clients in many locations. We prepare health records and statistics, and we work together with members of multidisciplinary health-care teams. 

Student: You mentioned services to clients. What is the nature of the service you provide directly to clients?

 

Respiratory Therapist: In providing services to clients, we must apply specialized knowledge and equipment. For example, in using a ventilator to physically assist a person’s breathing, we usually set a volume of gas that we want delivered. In doing this, we need to be aware that as the volume increases, the pressure in the lungs will go up. Lung disease may affect how quickly this pressure change can occur safely. The goal is to deliver enough volume to meet the needs of the individual while keeping the pressure to a minimum. By carefully controlling the pressure, damage to the lungs is avoided or, at least, minimized.

 

Student: What is the iron lung? Is it still used today to help patients breathe?

Respiratory Therapist: The iron lung enclosed a patient in a sealed steel drum with only the patient’s head and neck sticking out through an airtight collar. By increasing and decreasing the air pressure around the person’s body, the iron lung made the chest wall and diaphragm move in and out. Although the iron lung did help people breathe, it severely restricted mobility. Modern ventilators are less cumbersome and have taken the place of the iron lung.

Have you heard of hyperbaric chambers? They fully enclose the patient but are not meant to physically help the client breathe. In hyperbaric chambers the pressure is maintained at an elevated level rather than at the oscillating level used in iron lungs.

Student: How are hyperbaric chambers used in respiratory therapy?

Respiratory Therapist: Hyperbaric chambers are used to treat, say, someone who suffers from carbon monoxide poisoning. Treatment with hyperbaric oxygen is the delivery of oxygen at increased atmospheric pressure. This increase in pressure dissolves more oxygen into the body tissue and blood so that the body is saturated with more oxygen than normal to enhance healing. The duration of the treatment and the pressure within the hyperbaric chamber must be appropriate for the patient and his or her condition.

 

Student: Where do respiratory therapists work?

 

Respiratory Therapist: We work in a variety of settings: in hospitals, in homecare, and in the private sector. Some respiratory therapists decide to specialize with additional training and work as a cardiovascular perfusionist or a polysomnographic technologist, to name a couple.

Student: What personal characteristics are suited to this line of work?

Respiratory Therapist: This occupation is suited to all individuals who have a genuine interest in helping others and don’t mind working indoors.

Student: Where would you get the training to prepare you for this occupation?

Respiratory Therapist: Training is available through a number of universities and technical colleges across the country.

Student: Do I need to be licensed as a respiratory therapist?

Respiratory Therapist: Yes. After you’ve graduated from an approved educational institution, you must pass a provincial registration examination to become registered and work as a respiratory therapist.

Student: What employment opportunities are available?

Respiratory Therapist: A number of employment opportunities exist both locally and abroad in hospital and community settings.

Student: What is the salary range for a respiratory therapist?


Respiratory Therapist: The salary range is comparable to that of a school teacher or registered nurse. I find this occupation to be very satisfying. I receive a decent salary in a job that involves helping others.

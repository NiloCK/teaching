Look at the chlorine atom, and see how it becomes an ion.


In this animation you can watch the electron moving into the outer energy level of the chlorine atom. Once it gains the electron, there are 18 electrons and 17 protons. This excess of negative charges relative to positive charges results in a net charge of 1−, and the chlorine atom has now become a chloride ion. Notice that when non-metals become ions, the ending of their names is changed to -ide. Once the electron is added, this net charge is shown as Cl−.

 

An energy-level diagram of the chloride ion can also be drawn.

 

Notice that the electron configuration (the way the electrons are distributed in the energy levels) is now like argon—a noble gas. One final terminology note is that negatively charged ions are called anions.

 

anion: a negatively charged ion

 

 

 

But what happens if the atom is a metal? How does it turn into an ion?

 

All metals will lose electrons in their outer energy levels to be like the nearest noble gas. Look at aluminium, and see what happens to its electrons.

 


In this animation you can see the 3 electrons in the outer energy level of the aluminium atom. Because these 3 electrons are not bonded as strongly as the other 10 electrons, aluminium will lose these electrons to other atoms that have a strong attraction for them. As a result, the outer three electrons will be pulled off the atom to cause a charge imbalance. There are now 13 protons in the nucleus and only 10 electrons surrounding the nucleus, resulting in a net charge of 3+ for aluminium. When the 3 electrons leave the atom, aluminium becomes the aluminium ion and is written as Al3+.

 

A condensed version of the aluminium ion can also be drawn.

 

Notice that the aluminium ion has an energy level diagram similar to the noble gas neon. Positively charged ions are called cations.

 

cation: a positively charged ion
